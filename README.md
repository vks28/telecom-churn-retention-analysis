## Context
This project analyzes historical telecom customer data to understand why customers churn and what factors contribute to long-term retention. The analysis supports data-driven decision-making aimed at reducing churn and improving customer lifetime value.

**Data Source:** Public telecom customer churn dataset containing **7,043 customer records** collected over **three months**, including anonymized customer demographics, service subscriptions, contract details, and billing information.

---

## Analytical Approach
- Segmented customers into **churned vs. retained** groups  
- Analyzed churn and retention patterns across **tenure, contract type, services, and pricing**  
- Compared service adoption and billing behavior between churned and retained customers  
- Used interactive filtering to enable **root-cause exploration and drill-down analysis**

---

## Business Questions Addressed
- Which customer segments are most likely to churn?
- How do contract type and tenure influence churn risk?
- What services are commonly associated with retained customers?
- How does pricing impact customer loyalty?
- Where should retention efforts be focused?

---

## KPIs Tracked
- **Total Customers:** 7,043  
- **Churned Customers:** 1,869  
- **Churn Rate:** 26.5%  
- **Retained Customers:** 5,174  
- **Retention Rate:** 73.5%

---

## Key Insights

### Churn Insights
- **26.5% of customers churned**, with the highest churn among month-to-month contract customers.
- Customers with **short tenure (under one year)** show significantly higher churn risk.
- **Fiber optic customers churn at a higher rate** compared to DSL and non-internet users.
- Churn is not strongly driven by gender, indicating service and contract factors are more influential.

### Retention Insights
- **73.5% of customers are retained**, with stronger retention among long-tenure customers.
- One-year and two-year contracts show higher retention compared to month-to-month plans.
- Retained customers typically adopt more core services, suggesting higher engagement reduces churn risk.
- Mid-range pricing tiers show stronger retention than extreme low or high pricing segments.
